Problem Statement

Our group has been tasked with identifying a commonly used consumer product that demonstrates poor application of humanmachine interaction guidelines and principles that we have learned in this course. This presentation will examine Multi-Space Pay Stations that are being installed in cities across the United States as a replacement for single head parking meters. We will note that while these new parking systems may make parking easier to manage for municipalities, they do so at the expense of users. We will go on to identify HMI principles that these systems violate, and propose improved designs.

Features

  1. Reduced Cost in Maintenance
    • Pay stations need service twice a month compared with meters which require service up to twice each week, and reduce staff costs.
  2. Less down time
    • It is illegal to park at an inoperative/malfunctioning meter but if the pay station closest to you is inoperative, you can pay for your parking at ANY pay station and be on your way.
  3. Rate Flexibility
    • Multiple rates can be programmed into the units to allow for rate changes, i.e., all day rates, after hour rates, etc.
    • All rates can be programmed from a central computer instead of manually adjusting each individual meter.
  4. Less sidewalk clutter
    • Machines have a smaller footprint than single space meters, making for less obstruction in public walkways.
  5. Ability to use cloud-based payment systems (pay by smartphone)
    • Makes credit card processing simple, and allows users to add more time if they need more time than initially expected.
  6. Big Data
    • The information generated by these systems may give cities ideas to better serve citizens, see areas of greatest/least use, etc.
  7. Centralized operations and fund reporting
    • Management happens in one online system
