Street Parking:

Human Factors Case Study

Objective

In the fall of 2016, our student group was tasked with identifying a commonly-used consumer product that demonstrates poor application of human-machine interaction guidelines and principles. The resulting case study examined Multi-Space Pay Stations that are being installed in cities across the United States as a replacement for single-head parking meters.

While these new parking systems may make parking easier to manage for municipalities, they do so at the expense of users. Our analysis sought to identify the HMI principles that these systems violate and to propose improvements optimized for citizen use.

Team: Isaac Gertman, Thomas Jones

Analysis

User Goals

With a typical user flow in mind, we identified two simple but critical user goals: to spend little time completing a transaction and avoiding unnecessary costs (such as a fine from a ticket or accidentally paying too much).

This comes with one caveat: typically, a shorter transaction is better, but not always. For example, a quick transaction is pointless if you've paid for the wrong space or the wrong amount. So, the optimal customer experience would have a short duration and a low (zero) error rate.

Problem 1: Spacing

The average human walking speed is 3.1 mph (4.5 ft/sec), meaning that, at the longest distance, it could take 45 seconds or more to reach a kiosk.

Given that the upper capacity of short-term memory is 7 ± 2 characters and the fact that recall detoriates over time, it is probable that a user will forget their parking space number in that 45-second window, provided they do not engage in conscious subvocal maintainence rehearsal (better known by its scientific name, "muttering under your breath repeatedly").

Problem 2: Display Contrast

Contrast sensitivity is the minimum contrast between light and dark for the two areas to be differentiated from each other. In the case of a kiosk screen in the sunshine, the level of contrast is too low, which makes information harder to discern. Misreading the screen is an obvious source of error.

Solution

We proposed the addition of a manual brightness control to counter the glare of the sun. As contrast sensitivity degrades with age (less light passing through the cornea), this has the added benefit of increasing accessibility to better serve a diverse user population.

Problem 3: Feedback

In this interface, there is only one type of audio feedback for any given keystroke or alert. With its lack of variation, this audio feedback fails to distinguish between successes and errors.

A user's only recourse for interpreting the sound is to read the small text on the display to know what to do next, assuming it is legible at such low contrast.

Since the card slot is below a person's 30-35° range of vision, they must take their eyes off the screen to insert their card, forcing them to glance back and forth between the slot and the screen to know what to do next.

Solution

Suggestions:

  • Use symbolic sounds to differentiate meaning
  • Create redudancy between visual cues and auditory alerts
  • Reduce number of keystrokes, and therefore, transaction time
  • Decrease the angle the reader needs to adjust their vision to see the credit card slot

Conclusions

Hypotheses

  • Shorter distance between Car and Meter will reduce the amount of subvocal or mental rehearsal necessary to retain digits in working memory, thereby reducing error rate & transaction length.
  • Inputs with less digits or those arranged in ‘chunks’ will require less working memory capacity than a string of semantically unrelated digits and will therefore be easier to remember and enter correctly into the meter, thus reducing error rate & transaction duration.
  • The ability to adjust contrast and type size will increase the kiosks’ accessibility and customer satisfaction. The correlation between type size adjustment and satisfaction will be strongest in users with visual impairments, such as the elderly.
  • The presence of a progress bar will reduce instances of customers double paying for spaces, as they will receive visual confirmation that the transaction has been completed.
